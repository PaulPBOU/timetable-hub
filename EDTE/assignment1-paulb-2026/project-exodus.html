OPERATION EXODUS

Stage 3 Space Academy

Emergency Transmission

Earth is failing.
We must relocate.

THE CRISIS

Attention Junior Scientists. Due to severe global overheating, extreme pollution, and rapid overpopulation, our home planet is quickly becoming uninhabitable. The Global Space Agency has activated Operation Exodus.

We cannot stay here. We must look to the stars to survive. We need to find a new planet in our solar system to call home, design a way to transport humans there safely, and figure out how to survive on its alien surface.

Your Challenge

As lead aerospace engineers and planetary scientists, your mission is to answer this driving question:

"Which planet in our solar system can we relocate to, and how will we design the spacecraft and planetary systems to ensure human survival?"

Mission Specs

Your assessment requirements and success criteria.

What you need to do

To successfully complete Operation Exodus, you will complete three training activities. This will culminate in a final project showcase where you will present to the Global Space Agency (your classmates!).

You will present your planetary research, your spacecraft blueprint, and a physical 3D model of your chosen planet.

Learning Outcomes

  • ★ You can research and compare the environmental conditions of different planets in our solar system.
  • ★ You can design and draw a blueprint for a spacecraft that meets human biological needs.
  • ★ You can select and use sustainable, recycled materials to produce a 3D model.

The 3 Missions

Follow the Design Process to solve the relocation challenge. Detailed instructions below.

Identifying & Defining

Mission 1: Researching our New Home

We need to know where we are going! Because Earth is failing, you must select the most viable planet (or moon) in our solar system to relocate humanity. You will research planetary conditions and create a persuasive digital poster pitching your idea.

1

Data Gathering

Using your laptops, research the planets. You must record data on: Temperature, Atmosphere (is there oxygen?), Gravity, and Water presence. Use trusted sites like NASA Kids' Club.

2

Invent Solutions

No planet is perfect. Brainstorm inventive scientific solutions for your chosen planet's problems. (e.g. Venus is too hot? Design floating cloud-cities! Mars is dry? Giant ice-melting mirrors!).

3

The Digital Pitch

Open Canva or PowerPoint. Create a highly visual, persuasive "Destination Poster". Include your planetary facts, your inventive survival solutions, and bold graphics to convince the Agency.

💡
Commander's Hint

Your poster isn't just a list of facts. It's an advertisement! Use persuasive language like "Experience the endless energy of Titan's methane lakes!"

Researching & Planning

Mission 2: Designing the Ark

Now that we have a destination, how do we get humanity there safely? Space travel takes months or even years. You must design the transport spacecraft that will keep humans alive in the vacuum of space.

1

Biological Needs

In your science journals, list everything humans need for a 2-year journey: Oxygen generation, food/hydroponics, water recycling, artificial gravity (to stop bone loss), and radiation shielding.

2

Draft the Blueprint

Using A3 grid paper, rulers, and pencils, draw a detailed, scaled cross-section (inside view) of your spacecraft. Allocate zones: Engine room, sleeping quarters, bridge, and farming bays.

3

Label & Justify

Draw arrows to the technologies in your ship and label them. On the back of the paper, write a 100-word paragraph justifying why your layout keeps the crew safe and sane.

📐
Commander's Hint

Think about shape! Many spaceships use rotating circular rings to create artificial gravity. Check out designs from movies or real NASA concepts for inspiration before you draw.

Producing & Implementing

Mission 3: Building the New Planet

Earth is dying from waste and pollution, so our new society must be built entirely on sustainability. Your final task is to physically construct a 3D model of your chosen planet and human colony.

1

Material Scavenging

You may NOT buy new plastics. You must scavenge sustainable/recycled materials from home and school: Cardboard, egg cartons, plastic bottles, newspaper, and foil.

2

Construct the Surface

Use papier-mâché over balloons to create the planet globe, or use a flat cardboard base. Shape the geographical features you researched in Mission 1 (craters, mountains, ice caps).

3

Deploy the Colony

Integrate your human colony into the landscape. Glue clear plastic cups for biodomes. Use foil for solar arrays. Place it in the safest geographical zone (e.g. inside a crater for wind protection).

♻️
Commander's Hint

Be prepared to evaluate your work! During the final Expo, you will be asked to explain *why* your recycled materials were an effective choice for representing your high-tech colony.

Teacher Background Information

Curriculum mapping and theoretical frameworks.

Target Audience

Stage 3 (Yrs 5-6)

Syllabus

NSW NESA Sci & Tech

Strand

Earth and Space

Knowledge and Understanding

Investigates regular events in the solar system and geological events on the Earth's surface (ST3-10ES-S).

Task-Specific Elaboration: Researching and comparing the environmental conditions of different planets in our solar system to determine their viability for human relocation, and proposing scientific solutions to overcome extreme planetary conditions.

Processes and Production Skills

Plans and implements a design process, selecting a range of tools, equipment, materials and techniques to produce solutions (ST3-5WT).

Task-Specific Elaboration: Utilising the design process to ideate spacecraft transport blueprints and producing a physical 3D model of a new planetary colony using sustainable, recycled materials to solve a real-world crisis scenario.

Theoretical Underpinnings

This PBL sequence is scaffolded using Bloom's Taxonomy. Students begin at the understanding level (researching planetary facts in Activity 1), move to analysing (determining spacecraft needs in Activity 2), and culminate in creating (building the 3D model in Activity 3).

The task intentionally caters to Gardner's Multiple Intelligences. The digital pitch appeals to linguistic and visual-spatial learners, the spacecraft blueprint engages logical-mathematical intelligence, and the physical construction of the recycled planet strongly activates bodily-kinesthetic intelligence.

Assessment practices are multifaceted. Activities 1 and 2 serve as Assessment FOR Learning, allowing teachers to check misconceptions about space before the final build. The final 3D model acts as Assessment OF Learning, while the student reflection embedded in the success criteria ensures Assessment AS Learning.

Critical Reflection

Part B: Academic synthesis of pedagogical choices.

The "Operation Exodus" task positions Stage 3 students within an urgent, authentic real-world scenario—the ecological collapse of Earth—to drive engagement with the Earth and Space syllabus. This sequence is heavily underpinned by Project-Based Learning (PBL) pedagogies. By framing the unit around a complex challenge (relocating humanity), the task adheres to the Buck Institute for Education’s (2024) Gold Standard PBL framework, which emphasises a driving question, sustained inquiry, and a public product. Providing students with an apocalyptic but scientifically grounded scenario fosters high levels of authenticity and student agency, elements which Thomas (2000) identifies as critical for deep cognitive engagement in PBL.

To navigate this sustained inquiry, the sequence explicitly employs the Design Thinking process, closely mirroring the Design and Production skills within the NESA (2017) Science and Technology K-6 Syllabus. In Activity 1 (Identifying and defining), students empathise with the plight of climate refugees and research planetary conditions. This transitions into the ideation and planning phase (Activity 2), where cognitive scaffolding aids students in designing spacecraft blueprints that address human biological needs in a vacuum. Finally, Activity 3 (Producing and implementing) requires students to prototype their chosen planet using sustainable materials. As Bower (2017) notes, this iterative prototyping phase is vital in technology education, as it allows students to physically manipulate variables and solve problems in real-time.

The integration of digital technologies, such as utilising Canva for the planetary pitch in Activity 1, is justified through the Meaningful Learning Framework (Howland, Jonassen, & Marra, 2012). Here, technology is not used for passive consumption, but as an active, constructive tool for students to articulate their inventive scientific solutions. This represents the 'Modification' level of the SAMR model (Puentedura, 2006), as the digital format allows for the seamless integration of multimodal scientific data.

Finally, the task embeds Assessment as Learning by requiring students to reflect upon the efficacy of their recycled material choices. This metacognitive step ensures students actively evaluate their own design processes, solidifying their understanding of how sustainable design technologies can address complex scientific challenges.

G.S.A RESEARCH GUIDE

💧 Life Support Systems

G.S.A Data Extract

In the vacuum of space, nothing goes to waste. On the International Space Station (ISS), the Environmental Control and Life Support System (ECLSS) captures moisture from astronauts' breath and sweat, and even recycles urine back into clean drinking water! To get oxygen, ships run electricity through water (a process called electrolysis) to split it into hydrogen and breathable oxygen gas.

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    Where will you place the water recycling tanks on your ship? Does it need to be near the bathrooms or the engine?

  • ▶

    If your water recycler breaks down halfway to Jupiter, what emergency backup system could you design?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "How many litres of water does one astronaut need per day in space?"

🛡️ Hull & Shielding

G.S.A Data Extract

Space is highly dangerous. It is filled with micrometeoroids (tiny space rocks travelling up to 28,000 km/h) and deadly cosmic radiation from the Sun. Spacecraft hulls use multiple layers of advanced materials to survive. They use Aluminium and Titanium for structure, and layers of Kevlar (the material used in bulletproof vests) to stop high-speed impacts. Interestingly, liquid water is one of the best shields against radiation!

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    Why is it a bad idea to use heavy metals like Lead for radiation shielding on a rocket taking off from Earth?

  • ▶

    Since water blocks radiation well, could you line the walls of the crew's sleeping quarters with the ship's drinking water supply?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "What is the Whipple Shield on the International Space Station?"

🌱 Crew Wellbeing

G.S.A Data Extract

Travelling to a new planet will take months or years. In zero gravity, human bones and muscles quickly weaken, so astronauts must exercise for 2 hours every single day. Mentally, being trapped inside a metal tube is very stressful. To keep crews sane and happy, space agencies design recreational rooms, movie libraries, and even experimental greenhouses (like the "Veggie" project) so astronauts can care for living plants.

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    How could you design a spinning section of your ship to create 'artificial gravity' for the sleeping zone?

  • ▶

    What specific recreational activities will you include in your ship's layout to keep the crew entertained for 2 years?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "How do astronauts sleep and exercise on the ISS without floating away?"

🔭 Research & Exploration

G.S.A Data Extract

A space ark isn't just a transport ship; it's a mobile laboratory! Scientists need to study the stars, monitor cosmic weather, and scan their destination planet for safe landing zones. Ships are equipped with advanced sensor arrays, radar, and high-powered optical and infrared telescopes to look deep into space. They also carry deployable probes to test atmospheres before humans arrive.

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    Where will you position your telescopes and sensor arrays so they have an unobstructed view of space?

  • ▶

    How will your research lab safely communicate its findings back to Earth or the rest of the fleet?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "What instruments does the James Webb Space Telescope use to see distant planets?"

🍅 Food & Agriculture

G.S.A Data Extract

You can't pack enough freeze-dried space ice cream for a multi-year journey! Long-haul space travel requires sustainable food sources. Scientists use hydroponics and aeroponics to grow high-yield, nutrient-dense crops like kale, tomatoes, and potatoes without soil. These greenhouses also do double-duty by absorbing carbon dioxide and producing fresh oxygen for the crew!

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    How much of your ship's internal space will be dedicated to farming zones (hydroponics)?

  • ▶

    What kind of artificial lighting will your crops need since they are so far away from the Sun?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "How does NASA's Veggie project grow food on the International Space Station?"
