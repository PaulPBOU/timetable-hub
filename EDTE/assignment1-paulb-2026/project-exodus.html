THE CRISIS

Attention Junior Scientists. Due to severe global warming and overheating, pollution, and overpopulation, our beautiful blue home planet Earth is quickly becoming uninhabitable. The Global Space Agency has initiated Operation Exodus.

We cannot stay here. We must look to the other planets in the solar system to survive. We need to find a new planet in our solar system to call home, design a way to transport humans there safely, and figure out how to survive on its alien surface.

Your Challenge

As lead rocket scientists and planetary engineers, your mission is to answer these driving questions:

What if humans can't figure out how to work together to heal Earth?


"Which planet in our solar system can we relocate to, and how will we design the spacecraft and planetary systems to ensure human survival?"

YOUR 3 MISSIONS

Follow the sequence to complete your missions and help humans relocate.

Identifying & Defining

MISSION ONE: Researching our New Home

Where are humans going? You need to conduct research to find the most suitable planet in our solar system. 

1

Data Gathering

Using your laptops, research the planets. On a new page in your books, you need to write about: Temperature, Atmosphere (is there oxygen?), Gravity, and Water presence. Use the provided planet database by clicking:

2

Invent Solutions

Every planet is different. Brainstorm inventive scientific solutions for your chosen planet's problems. (e.g. Venus is too hot? Design floating cloud cities. Mars is dry? Giant mirrors that melt ice into water). Be creative. 

3

The Presentation

Using Canva, Google Slides or PowerPoint: Create a highly visual, persuasive presentation on your chosen planet. Include your planetary facts, your inventive survival solutions, and creative graphics to convince everyone.

💡
Hint

Your presentation isn't just a list of facts. It's a persuasive advertisement. Use language like "Bathe in the cool thermal waters of Venus' rock pools."

Researching & Planning

MISSION TWO: Designing the Spaceship

Now that we have chosen a planet, how do we get humans there safely? Space travel takes months or even years. You need to design the spaceship that will keep humans alive in the deadly vacuum of space.

1

Biological Needs

In your science books, list everything humans need for a 2 year journey: Oxygen generation, food/hydroponics, water recycling, artificial gravity and radiation shielding. Use the research guide linked below to start!

2

Draft the Blueprint

Using A3 grid paper, rulers, and pencils, draw a detailed view of the inside of your spacecraft. It should have zones like: Engine room, sleeping quarters, bridge, and farming areas. See the sketch samples for inspiration.

3

Label & Justify

Draw arrows to the technologies in your ship and label them. Under your blueprint, write a 100-word paragraph justifying why/how your ship's layout keeps humans safe and sane.

📐
Hint

Think about shapes...spaceships use rotating rings to create gravity. Check out designs from movies or real NASA concepts for inspiration before you draw.

Producing & Implementing

MISSION THREE: Surviving on the new planet

Pollution and waste is a problem on Earth, so our new society needs to be built sustainably. Your final task is to physically construct a 3D model of your chosen planet and human colony.

1

Material Scavenging

Please do NOT purchase new materials. You should scavenge sustainable/recycled materials: Cardboard, egg cartons, plastic bottles, newspaper, and foil. See the recycling breifing below for more information.

2

Construct the Surface

Use paper-mache over balloons to create the planet globe, or use a flat cardboard base. Shape the geographical features you researched in Mission 1 (craters, mountains, ice caps) using your recycled materials.

3

Deploy the Colony

Add your human colony into the landscape and other structures they will need. Use foil for solar arrays. Use toothpicks and paper to LABEL each important structure. 

Write 100 words to justify your design choices.

♻️
Hint

Write detailed notes on your design choices during construction. You will need these notes to explain why  your recycled materials were an effective choice for representing your human colony and structures.

Curriculum mapping and theoretical frameworks.

Target Audience

Knowledge and Understanding

Investigates regular events in the solar system and geological events on the Earth's surface (ST3-10ES-S).

Task-Specific Elaboration: Researching and comparing the environmental conditions of different planets in our solar system to determine their viability for human relocation, and proposing scientific solutions to overcome extreme planetary conditions.

Processes and Production Skills

Plans and implements a design process, selecting a range of tools, equipment, materials and techniques to produce solutions (ST3-5WT).

Task-Specific Elaboration: Utilising the design process to ideate spacecraft transport blueprints and producing a physical 3D model of a new planetary colony using sustainable, recycled materials to solve a real-world crisis scenario.

Theoretical Underpinnings

This PBL sequence is scaffolded using Bloom's Taxonomy. Students begin at the understanding level (researching planetary facts in Activity 1), move to analysing (determining spacecraft needs in Activity 2), and culminate in creating (building the 3D model in Activity 3).

The task intentionally caters to Gardner's Multiple Intelligences. The digital pitch appeals to linguistic and visual-spatial learners, the spacecraft blueprint engages logical-mathematical intelligence, and the physical construction of the recycled planet strongly activates bodily-kinesthetic intelligence.

Assessment practices are multifaceted. Activities 1 and 2 serve as Assessment FOR Learning, allowing teachers to check misconceptions about space before the final build. The final 3D model acts as Assessment OF Learning, while the student reflection embedded in the success criteria ensures Assessment AS Learning.

Planetary Facts 📊

🌡️ Temperature

Super freezing! It drops to -179°C. We can't step outside without heavy heated suits.

☁️ Atmosphere

Really thick and mostly nitrogen (like Earth!). This acts as an amazing shield against radiation.

⚖️ Gravity

Very low! Because the air is so thick and gravity is low, humans could strap on wings and fly!

💧 Water

Huge lakes of liquid methane and solid water-ice rocks covering the surface.

How Will We Survive? 🛠️

⚠️ Hazard 1: It is way too cold!

Our Solution: We will build huge insulated biodomes. We can heat them by burning the unlimited liquid methane fuel we scoop right out of the lakes! Endless energy.

⚠️ Hazard 2: We can't breathe the air!

Our Solution: We will use mining rovers to dig up the solid water-ice rocks. We will melt them and use electricity to split the water into oxygen for our domes to breathe!

Sustainable Food 🍅

The Hydroponic Solution

Because the journey and setup will take years, we must establish sustainable food sources. Our colony will feature multi-level hydroponic farming towers. These will grow high-yield, nutrient-dense crops like tomatoes and kale without relying on soil. They also act as natural air filters, turning carbon dioxide back into oxygen!

Colony Infrastructure 🏗️

Safe and Secure Shelters

To build our new homes, we will utilise robust 3D-printed structures made from locally sourced ice and regolith. The thick atmosphere of Titan will protect us from radiation, while the heavy heated suits will keep us safe from the freezing surface. Operation Exodus will be a success!

Why Titan Beats Mars 🚀

  • 🛡️ Mars has a thin atmosphere so dangerous radiation gets in. Titan has a thick atmosphere that acts as a natural shield!
  • ⛽ Mars has no fuel. Titan is literally covered in oceans of methane fuel we can use to power our cities!
  • 🗳️ Vote for Titan to save humanity!

RESEARCH GUIDE

💧 Life Support Systems

Data Extract

In the vacuum of space, nothing goes to waste. On the International Space Station (ISS), the Environmental Control and Life Support System (ECLSS) captures moisture from astronauts' breath and sweat, and even recycles urine back into clean drinking water! To get oxygen, ships run electricity through water (a process called electrolysis) to split it into hydrogen and breathable oxygen gas.

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    Where will you place the water recycling tanks on your ship? Does it need to be near the bathrooms or the engine?

  • ▶

    If your water recycler breaks down halfway to Jupiter, what emergency backup system could you design?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "How many litres of water does one astronaut need per day in space?"

🛡️ Hull & Shielding

Data Extract

Space is highly dangerous. It is filled with micrometeoroids (tiny space rocks travelling up to 28,000 km/h) and deadly cosmic radiation from the Sun. Spacecraft hulls use multiple layers of advanced materials to survive. They use Aluminium and Titanium for structure, and layers of Kevlar (the material used in bulletproof vests) to stop high-speed impacts. Interestingly, liquid water is one of the best shields against radiation!

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    Why is it a bad idea to use heavy metals like Lead for radiation shielding on a rocket taking off from Earth?

  • ▶

    Since water blocks radiation well, could you line the walls of the crew's sleeping quarters with the ship's drinking water supply?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "What is the Whipple Shield on the International Space Station?"

🌱 Crew Wellbeing

Data Extract

Travelling to a new planet will take months or years. In zero gravity, human bones and muscles quickly weaken, so astronauts must exercise for 2 hours every single day. Mentally, being trapped inside a metal tube is very stressful. To keep crews sane and happy, space agencies design recreational rooms, movie libraries, and even experimental greenhouses (like the "Veggie" project) so astronauts can care for living plants.

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    How could you design a spinning section of your ship to create 'artificial gravity' for the sleeping zone?

  • ▶

    What specific recreational activities will you include in your ship's layout to keep the crew entertained for 2 years?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "How do astronauts sleep and exercise on the ISS without floating away?"

🔭 Research & Exploration

Data Extract

A space ark isn't just a transport ship; it's a mobile laboratory! Scientists need to study the stars, monitor cosmic weather, and scan their destination planet for safe landing zones. Ships are equipped with advanced sensor arrays, radar, and high-powered optical and infrared telescopes to look deep into space. They also carry deployable probes to test atmospheres before humans arrive.

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    Where will you position your telescopes and sensor arrays so they have an unobstructed view of space?

  • ▶

    How will your research lab safely communicate its findings back to Earth or the rest of the fleet?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "What instruments does the James Webb Space Telescope use to see distant planets?"

🍅 Food & Agriculture

Data Extract

You can't pack enough freeze-dried space ice cream for a multi-year journey! Long-haul space travel requires sustainable food sources. Scientists use hydroponics and aeroponics to grow high-yield, nutrient-dense crops like kale, tomatoes, and potatoes without soil. These greenhouses also do double-duty by absorbing carbon dioxide and producing fresh oxygen for the crew!

Guiding Questions for your Blueprint
  • ▶

    How much of your ship's internal space will be dedicated to farming zones (hydroponics)?

  • ▶

    What kind of artificial lighting will your crops need since they are so far away from the Sun?

  • ▶

    Web Search: "How does NASA's Veggie project grow food on the International Space Station?"
